What is the CoDesign Tool?

This page presents the methodology behind the tool: its conceptual foundations, the seven-stage process, the cognitive-emotional design principles that make it specific to cultural heritage, and the two formats it comes in.

What it is

A card-and-board framework for co-design

Cultural heritage projects are inherently interdisciplinary. Curators, technologists, educators, and designers must work together, but they rarely share a common design vocabulary. The CoDesign Tool provides that common ground: a modular, game-like system of boards and card decks that structures the entire design process from the first question to the final storyboard.

Developed within the Horizon Europe PERCEIVE project by CNR ISPC (Italy) and Fraunhofer IGD (Germany), it builds on the legacy of the open-access VisitorBox toolkit and extends it with cognitive-emotional design dimensions specific to the heritage domain.

Why it was built

The problems it was built to solve

These challenges are not unique to any one institution or project. They reflect the structural complexity of designing for cultural heritage: a field that requires both disciplinary breadth and emotional depth, under real constraints of time, budget, and expertise. The CoDesign Tool was built to address them directly, without requiring teams to become design experts first.

Participation barriers between disciplines

Heritage teams mix professionals who rarely share design methods. Cards lower the entry threshold: anyone can contribute regardless of their background.

Sessions that run long without a result

Without structure, co-design sessions drift. The CoDesign Tool keeps teams moving: in evaluation, groups completed the full process two hours faster than with the original VisitorBox.

Missing the visitor's emotional experience

Most design tools focus on features and functions. The CoDesign Tool includes a dedicated stage for cognitive-emotional goals, Authenticity and Sense of Care, that are central to meaningful heritage experiences.

Who uses it

Research consortia

Multi-institutional teams like PERCEIVE use it to co-design proof-of-concept experiences across distributed partners, aligning goals before development begins.

University courses

Used in Master-level programmes in Digital Humanities and Multimodal Design to teach interdisciplinary design processes in an interactive, hands-on format.

Museums and heritage institutions

Curators, educators, and technologists working together to design digital or hybrid visitor experiences, without relying on external design consultants.

Design sprints and workshops

Any team that needs to move from a blank slate to a tested, storyboarded concept in a single session, whether in a classroom, a museum lab, or a conference workshop.

The process

Seven stages from context to storyboard

The process is sequential and divided into two phases. The first four stages build a complete Design Brief. The Design Brief then becomes the foundation for the second phase, where the team generates, visualises, and stress-tests their concept.

Phase 1 / Design Brief
01
Context
Who and what
Define who this experience is for and what it revolves around.

The team identifies the heritage asset at the centre of the project and the audience it wants to reach. This shared starting point prevents misalignment later: everyone is designing for the same person, the same collection, the same site.

02
Institutional Goals
What success looks like
Align on what the institution needs this experience to achieve.

Cultural institutions have specific goals that go beyond visitor satisfaction: conservation, education, community engagement. This stage surfaces those goals explicitly so they shape every design decision that follows.

03
Audience Goals
Authenticity and Sense of Care
Define the cognitive and emotional impact the experience should have on visitors.

This is the stage unique to the CoDesign Tool. Two dedicated decks, Authenticity and Sense of Care, translate research-grounded goals into concrete design directions.

04
Design Brief
Everything converges here
Converge the previous three stages into a single shared brief.

The Design Brief is both a stage and a milestone. The first three canvases stack on top of the Design Brief Board, building the brief incrementally. By the time the team reaches this point, the brief is already complete and ready for ideation.

The Design Brief becomes the foundation for Phase 2
Phase 2 / Ideation and Storyboard
05
Ideation
Concepts and directions
Generate and develop design concepts as a team.

With the Design Brief in hand, the team uses Ideation Cards to explore concepts. The cards prompt creative directions and ensure that the cognitive-emotional goals from Stage 3 are reflected in the ideas being developed.

06
Disruption
Threat, recommendation, question
Stress-test the concept before committing to it.

Teams draw scenario cards across three categories (Engagement, Environment, Resilience) that expose vulnerabilities. Each card presents a threat, a recommendation, and a reflective question. In evaluation, 86% of participants revised their concept after this stage.

07
Storyboard
The final deliverable
Visualise the final concept as a shareable storyboard.

The team translates their refined concept into a visual narrative: a sequence of scenes that shows how a visitor would experience the designed application. The storyboard is shareable with stakeholders and archivable for future reference.

Audience Goals

Audience Goals: the cognitive-emotional layer

Most design frameworks focus on what visitors do. The CoDesign Tool also asks what visitors feel, understand, and carry away. Stage 3 introduces two dedicated card decks developed within PERCEIVE, each grounded in academic research on cultural heritage experience design.

Authenticity deck, example cards

Authenticity

Explores how visitors connect emotionally, cognitively, and socially with a heritage experience. Organised around three dimensions (the Self, the Others, and the World), it translates philosophical concepts of authenticity into concrete design actions.

  • Self: personal connection and meaning
  • Others: social and relational experience
  • World: spatial and contextual engagement
Sense of Care deck, example cards

Sense of Care

Addresses the relationship between visitors and cultural heritage as one of active care and responsibility. Draws on Tronto's philosophy of care and PERCEIVE's own research framework, translating them into prompts and ideation directions.

  • Historical empathy and dialogue
  • Participatory and collaborative practices
  • Narrative strategies for urgency and loss

Both decks are grounded in peer-reviewed research developed within the PERCEIVE project.

System design

Built to be flexible

The CoDesign Tool is not a fixed script. Stages and decks can both be adapted to fit a specific context.

Use the stages you need

The seven stages reflect a classic design process. You can run them all, or pick the phase that fits your situation and hop in at any point. Already have a Design Brief? Jump straight to Ideation. Only need to stress-test an existing concept? Run Disruption on its own.

Paper vs Web App

Choose your format

The framework is the same. The format depends on where and how your team works.

Image placeholder
Best for

Paper version

When your team is in the same room. Physical cards and boards create a tangible, collaborative experience that encourages equal participation and keeps everyone focused.

  • In-person workshops and design sprints
  • Situations where screens would be a distraction
  • Teams new to co-design methods
  • Contexts where haptic engagement matters
Get started with paper
Image placeholder
Best for

Web App

When your team is distributed across locations or institutions. The web app preserves the collaborative dynamics of the physical tool while enabling remote participation and digital documentation.

  • Remote and hybrid design sessions
  • International research consortia
  • When you need to share and archive results digitally
  • Follow-up sessions after an initial in-person workshop
